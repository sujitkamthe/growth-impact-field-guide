## Explorer Calibration Examples

### Reflection Prompts

- When I finished my last task, did I need someone to check each step, or did I work through it with only occasional guidance?
- If my assigned teammate was unavailable for a day, would my work continue or stall?
- Am I learning the "why" behind practices, or just following instructions?
- Do I proactively communicate my progress, or does my team have to check on me?

### Below Expectations

**Example 1:** You were assigned a well-defined story with clear acceptance criteria. You needed step-by-step walkthroughs at each stage and couldn't proceed when your buddy was unavailable. The story took significantly longer than estimated, and the final implementation needed substantial rework after review.

**Example 2:** You're completing tasks, but only in very controlled conditions. When the requirements shifted slightly mid-sprint, you couldn't adjust without detailed re-explanation. You rarely ask questions proactively. Instead, issues surface during code review or testing.

### Meets Expectations

**Example 1:** You picked up a well-scoped story and completed it with minimal guidance. You asked a few clarifying questions upfront, used existing code patterns as reference, and delivered working code that passed review with minor suggestions. When you hit a blocker, you attempted to debug it before asking for help.

**Example 2:** You set up your local environment with some help, but now you can run, test, and debug independently. You've started understanding the codebase well enough to know where to look when something breaks. Your PR feedback is getting lighter each sprint.

### Exceeds Expectations

**Example 1:** You completed your assigned tasks reliably and also started helping a newer teammate navigate the codebase. You documented a setup issue that had tripped up multiple people. Teammates noted you were ramping up faster than expected and starting to catch your own bugs before review.

**Example 2:** You not only delivered your stories on time, but proactively identified a small improvement to an existing workflow and raised it with the team. You're asking "why" questions that show genuine curiosity about the system, not just the task. Your team lead mentioned they've started giving you slightly less guidance because you don't need it.

## Artisan Calibration Examples

### Reflection Prompts

- Do I own stories end-to-end, or do I still need someone to define the approach for me?
- When I look at my recent work, is my impact limited to my own tasks, or does it extend to the team's quality?
- Would my teammates say I make the codebase better, not just add to it?
- Am I starting to shape solutions, or am I still primarily executing what's defined?

### Below Expectations

**Example 1:** You deliver your stories, but they frequently require significant rework after review. You're writing code that works but isn't clean or maintainable. You don't write tests unless explicitly asked, and when requirements are slightly ambiguous, you implement your assumption without checking.

**Example 2:** You've been in the role long enough that people expect independent delivery, but you still need others to break down stories into implementation steps. Your estimates are consistently off, and when deadlines approach, the team has to step in to help finish your work.

### Meets Expectations

**Example 1:** You pick up a feature story, break it down yourself, implement it end-to-end with tests, and deliver it on time. Your code is clean and your PRs get approved with minor comments. When you see something unclear in the requirements, you flag it early and suggest alternatives rather than guessing.

**Example 2:** You've been consistently delivering features with good quality. You refactored a messy module while working on a related story, not because anyone asked, but because you saw it needed it. Your code reviews for others are specific and helpful, catching real issues rather than just style nitpicks.

### Exceeds Expectations

**Example 1:** Beyond delivering your own work well, you identified a recurring pattern of bugs in a specific area and proposed a solution that the team adopted. Your PR reviews have noticeably improved the code quality of less experienced teammates. The tech lead mentioned they trust you to own features without check-ins.

**Example 2:** You took ownership of a complex story that crossed multiple services, coordinated with the relevant people, and delivered it without needing anyone to manage the dependencies for you. You also started questioning the business rationale behind a feature and suggested a simpler approach that saved the team two sprints of work.

## Catalyst Calibration Examples

### Reflection Prompts

- When I step out of a project for a week, does progress continue at the same pace, or does ambiguity creep back in?
- Am I shaping solutions and approaches, or am I still primarily building them myself?
- Do people come to me because they trust my judgment on complex problems, or just because I'm available?
- Have I improved how the team works, or only how I work?

### Below Expectations

**Example 1:** You're technically strong and deliver well, but your impact is still mostly limited to your own code. When the team faces ambiguity, you wait for someone else to frame the problem. You attend design discussions but contribute solutions rather than shaping the approach.

**Example 2:** You've taken on more responsibility, but you're acting as a hub rather than enabling the team. People route decisions through you because it's faster, but the team hasn't become more capable at making those decisions independently. When you were on leave, two decisions stalled until you returned.

### Meets Expectations

**Example 1:** You led the technical design for a complex feature, breaking down an ambiguous requirement into a clear approach the team could execute. You facilitated the design discussion, documented the trade-offs, and helped the team understand why you chose this approach over alternatives. The implementation went smoothly because the thinking was done upfront.

**Example 2:** You noticed the team was spending too much time on a recurring type of bug. Instead of just fixing each instance, you identified the root cause, proposed an architectural change, and coached two teammates through implementing it. The class of bug has since disappeared. You also mentored a junior teammate who is now handling similar analysis independently.

### Exceeds Expectations

**Example 1:** You redefined the approach to a client engagement that was struggling with scope creep. You facilitated a workshop that reframed the problem, aligned three stakeholders with competing priorities, and produced a roadmap the team delivered against for two quarters. The client specifically credited the clarity you brought.

**Example 2:** You identified that the team's testing strategy was creating a false sense of confidence. You proposed and drove a shift from unit-test-heavy to contract-test-driven approach, coached the team through the transition, and reduced production incidents by 40%. Other teams have since adopted the same approach, citing your documentation as the starting point.

## Multiplier Calibration Examples

### Reflection Prompts

- If I stepped away from my team for a month, would they maintain quality and momentum, or would things degrade?
- Am I building the team's capability, or am I the team's capability?
- Do I measure my success by my own output, or by how independently effective my team has become?
- Is the team's consulting, delivery, and technical quality improving because of systems I've put in place, or because I'm personally involved in everything?

### Below Expectations

**Example 1:** You're leading the team, but you're the single point of contact for all technical decisions and client interactions. When you're unavailable, the team waits. You're doing great individual work, but you haven't built the team's capacity to operate without you. Your "delegation" means assigning tasks, not transferring context and judgment.

**Example 2:** You've mentored individuals, but the team's overall capability hasn't improved measurably. Team members still escalate the same types of problems they did six months ago. The delivery practices you introduced are followed only when you enforce them. When a teammate left, the knowledge gap they left was larger than expected.

### Meets Expectations

**Example 1:** Your team delivers consistently and handles most decisions independently. You've built a team where two members can engage clients directly, technical decisions happen without waiting for you, and delivery is predictable. When you took a two-week break, the team maintained their pace and quality. You focused your return on coaching, not catching up on decisions.

**Example 2:** You identified that the team's consulting capability was a bottleneck and systematically addressed it. You paired with team members during client calls, gave them feedback after, and gradually reduced your direct involvement. Three team members now lead client conversations independently. You also introduced a delivery retrospective format that the team continued using after you stopped facilitating it.

### Exceeds Expectations

**Example 1:** Your team has become a reference point for the office. Other team leads seek your advice on team-building practices. Two people you mentored have taken on team lead responsibilities themselves. The client extended the engagement specifically citing the team's capability, not just your presence. Your practices for onboarding, code review, and client engagement have been adopted by another team.

**Example 2:** You transformed a struggling team into one that delivers reliably and consults effectively. You built systems for knowledge sharing, established clear escalation paths, and created a culture where team members proactively mentor each other. The team's delivery metrics improved significantly, and more importantly, those improvements persisted through a period when half the team rotated to other projects.

## Amplifier Calibration Examples

### Reflection Prompts

- Are the platforms, tools, or standards I've created actually being used by others, or do they mostly solve my own problems?
- If I stopped maintaining what I've built, would it continue to provide value, or would it decay?
- Am I measuring impact by adoption and outcomes, or by the sophistication of what I've built?
- Does my work enable teams to succeed independently, or does it create a new dependency on me?

### Below Expectations

**Example 1:** You built an internal tool that solves a real problem, but only your team uses it. Others tried it and found the documentation insufficient or the setup too complex. You're maintaining it yourself, and when you're busy, teams go back to their old workflows. The tool is technically impressive but practically underadopted.

**Example 2:** You created coding standards and guidelines, but they exist mostly as documents that people reference when reminded. Adoption is inconsistent. Teams follow the standards when you review their work, but drift when you're not involved. You haven't figured out how to embed the standards into tooling or workflows that enforce them automatically.

### Meets Expectations

**Example 1:** You built a shared library that three teams adopted for handling a common cross-cutting concern. The documentation is clear enough that teams onboarded without your help. You gathered feedback, iterated on the design, and it's now part of the standard project setup. Teams contribute back fixes and improvements without your involvement.

**Example 2:** You identified that teams were spending significant time on environment setup and CI/CD configuration. You created a platform template that reduced new project setup from days to hours. Four teams have used it, and the template has been maintained by others who added their own improvements. You also wrote a technical blog post about the approach that was well-received externally.

### Exceeds Expectations

**Example 1:** You created a testing framework that fundamentally changed how teams write and run tests across Sahaj. It reduced test suite execution time by 60% and increased adoption of integration testing from 2 teams to all active projects. Other engineers have contributed significant features to the framework. The approach has been presented at two external conferences by different Sahaj engineers, not just you.

**Example 2:** You identified a pattern of production incidents related to deployment practices and built an observability platform that teams now rely on for every release. It's become standard infrastructure at Sahaj. You designed it to be self-service from the start. Teams configure their own dashboards and alerts without needing your help. The platform has prevented several potential outages, and the approach has influenced how new client engagements set up their monitoring.

## Strategist Calibration Examples

### Reflection Prompts

- Is my impact measured across accounts and the organisation, or is it still concentrated within a single team or project?
- Am I shaping Sahaj's direction and reputation, or am I executing well within a scope someone else defined?
- Would my peers say I think in terms of what's right for Sahaj long-term, or what's needed for the current engagement?
- Are the decisions I make creating durable change, or are they being revisited after I move on?

### Below Expectations

**Example 1:** You're leading an account effectively, but your impact hasn't extended beyond it. You make strong decisions for your client, but you haven't shaped how Sahaj approaches similar problems elsewhere. When asked about organisational priorities, your perspective is anchored in your current engagement rather than Sahaj's broader trajectory.

**Example 2:** You've set a direction for your account, but it exists mostly in conversations and decks rather than in changed behaviour. Teams interpret your strategy differently depending on who you've spoken to. The direction hasn't survived contact with trade-offs. When delivery pressures mount, the strategy gets quietly abandoned.

### Meets Expectations

**Example 1:** You've shaped the technical and consulting approach across two accounts, and the patterns you established are being referenced by teams in a third. You identified a gap in Sahaj's service offering, built a case for it, and piloted it with a willing client. The pilot succeeded and is being expanded. You also sponsored two high-potential engineers into roles they wouldn't have reached without your support.

**Example 2:** You led a strategic shift in how Sahaj engages with a key client, moving from project-based delivery to embedded partnership. This required aligning multiple stakeholders, redefining team structures, and changing how success is measured. The shift has resulted in a more sustainable engagement model and has become a reference for how Sahaj approaches similar accounts.

### Exceeds Expectations

**Example 1:** You defined a technical capability area for Sahaj that didn't previously exist, built the team around it, and secured three engagements based on it. This has become a recognised strength that Sahaj markets externally. You also shaped the organisational culture around knowledge sharing in this area, ensuring the capability isn't dependent on you personally.

**Example 2:** You identified an industry shift early, convinced leadership to invest in building capability ahead of demand, and positioned Sahaj as a thought leader in the space. This resulted in inbound client interest, conference speaking invitations, and a talent pipeline of people who wanted to join Sahaj specifically for this work. The strategy you set is now being executed by others, and it's producing results two years after you initiated it.

## Pioneer Calibration Examples

### Reflection Prompts

- Is my external visibility translating into internal impact, or are they disconnected?
- Am I creating innovations that others adopt, or am I innovating in isolation?
- Would Sahaj's technical reputation in my domain area weaken noticeably if I left?
- Do my ideas change how people work, or are they admired but not applied?

### Below Expectations

**Example 1:** You're technically deep and have spoken at conferences, but the content is loosely connected to Sahaj's work. Internally, teams don't reference your talks or ideas. Your innovation work produced interesting prototypes, but none translated into practical tools or approaches that teams adopted.

**Example 2:** You have strong external recognition, but internally your influence is limited to your immediate project. When asked to help shape Sahaj's technical direction, your contributions are based on personal expertise rather than a strategic view of where Sahaj should go. Your thought leadership hasn't created a followership.

### Meets Expectations

**Example 1:** You pioneered an approach to a technical challenge that became the standard at Sahaj. You published about it, spoke at conferences, and the approach was adopted by three client engagements. Internally, you created reference implementations that teams use as starting points. Engineers in related areas seek your input, and your ideas have measurably changed how Sahaj delivers in this domain.

**Example 2:** You identified an emerging technology trend early and built Sahaj's capability around it. You created learning paths, mentored engineers interested in the area, and secured two engagements where this expertise was the differentiator. Your external writing has generated inbound interest, and your internal contributions have raised the bar for technical depth in this area.

### Exceeds Expectations

**Example 1:** You've become an industry-recognised voice in your domain. Your work has influenced how the broader community approaches a specific class of problems. Internally, the capability you built has become a revenue driver and a talent attractor. You've created a self-sustaining ecosystem of engineers who carry this work forward. The innovation pipeline doesn't depend on you.

**Example 2:** You defined a new practice area for Sahaj that sits at the intersection of two emerging trends. Your thought leadership shaped not just Sahaj's approach, but influenced how clients and the broader industry think about the problem space. Multiple Sahaj engineers have built on your work to develop their own expertise, speak at conferences, and lead engagements. The impact compounds well beyond your direct involvement.
